\chapter{Experiment Results}

Welcome to the finish line! In this chapter, we get to see the fruits of our labor. Did all of that complex GPU coding actually pay off? We will explain how we tested our new OptiX pipeline, compare its speed against the older PyMeshLab method, and look at some beautiful heat maps to see if the quality holds up!

\section{Testing Method}

To test our code, we ran a race! We pitted our new OptiX approach against the existing PyMeshLab camera-trick method. To make it a fair fight, we gave them both the exact same settings: shoot 64 rays per point, use a 150-degree wide cone, and take 10 layers of depth peeling. We then used a stopwatch (\texttt{std::chrono}) to see who crossed the finish line first. 

Once the numbers were crunched, we used a 3D viewer called Polyscope to paint the 3D models with a "turbo" colormap. Blue means the model is thin at that point, and red means it is thick. We'll show three images for every model: our OptiX result, the PyMeshLab result, and a "Difference" map that highlights any discrepancies between the two.

\section{Time Complexity and Speed Comparison}

Before we look at the stopwatch, let's talk about the math behind the race. 

Our OptiX pipeline relies on building a "Guess Who" box (BVH), which takes $O(F \log F)$ time. Once built, finding a hit for a single ray takes only $O(\log F)$ time. If we have $V$ vertices and shoot $R$ rays per vertex (in our case, 64), the ray tracing complexity is $O(V \times R \times \log F)$. Thus, the overall theoretical time complexity of the OptiX pipeline is $O(V \times R \times \log F + F \log F)$.

On the other hand, the PyMeshLab method has to render the entire model over and over again from $N$ different camera angles (in our case, 64). For each angle, it must perform $P$ layers of depth peeling (we use 10). Crucially, this $P$ parameter cannot be found automatically; the user must manually guess and tune the number of peeling iterations to ensure the model's inner layers are accurately captured. This means its theoretical time complexity is $O(N \times (F \times P + V))$, which simplifies to roughly $O(640 \times F)$ just for the rendering portion!

So why does OptiX absolutely crush PyMeshLab? First, OptiX uses dedicated RT Core hardware to test ray hits instantly, whereas PyMeshLab uses software draw calls. Second, OptiX keeps all the data on the super-fast GPU memory, while PyMeshLab forces the CPU and GPU to constantly pass data back and forth, and overall complexity is reduced. Moreover, we don't need to waste time tuning the P parameter.

Take a look at Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}. The results speak for themselves! OptiX is faster on every single model, with speedups ranging from a respectable 3.1x all the way to a mind-blowing 171.0x faster!

\section{Overall Conclusion}

So, what did we learn? We achieved a massive 171x speedup, but did we sacrifice quality? Absolutely not! In fact, the OptiX thickness maps look significantly better, smoother, and more realistic than the PyMeshLab results! 

Why? Because PyMeshLab just spits out raw, noisy numbers. Our OptiX pipeline uses the clever "dimmer-switch" smoothing filter to clean up the noise while perfectly preserving sharp edges. This project proves that using dedicated ray tracing hardware (OptiX) isn't just faster—it's fundamentally better!
